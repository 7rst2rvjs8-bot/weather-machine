What’s measured · the foundations

Three assumptions, tested.

Before building further, three big assumptions were checked against real data. Each came back as a finding that reshaped the plan rather than confirming it. They’re written here at the honest limit of what’s actually known — not the flattering version.

01  /  The findings
Does the strategy really have an edge?
MAYBE — NOT PROVEN. After fairly subtracting the luck of picking the best-looking option, a small advantage still seems to survive. But that number is a best case, not a verdict: three bigger doubts are still open — hidden fine-tuning, a “couldn’t really have known to pick these” problem, and a limited budget of honest tests. Not disproven is not the same as proven.
Can it really trade at the prices it assumes?
SPEED IS A THREAT — AND BEING RE-MEASURED. An early estimate of the hidden cost of being a moment too slow turned out to be partly a measurement glitch, so that number is being recomputed, not trusted. What does hold: a roughly fixed cost hurts cheap trades far more than expensive ones — pointing toward only trading above a price floor. Why this matters →
Are its five test-streams really five different bets?
NO — MORE LIKE TWO-AND-A-BIT. Small variations of a single idea barely count as separate bets. Real variety needs genuinely different ideas, not more tweaks of the same one. What a test-stream is →

02  /  The patience finding

Proving one fix takes months, not weeks.

At the current rate of trades, proving a single small improvement to a real standard of confidence needs hundreds to a couple thousand future trades — on the order of seven months to a year and a half of patient waiting, per fix. That’s the real price of honesty here, and it’s why the system is built to keep what proves itself early and drop what fails early, rather than waiting out a fixed clock.

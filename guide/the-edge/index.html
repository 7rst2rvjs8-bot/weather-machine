What “an edge” means.

“Edge” is a plain question dressed in a fancy word: over many, many trades, does this actually come out ahead, after all the costs? For this system, the honest answer right now is — we don’t know yet.

Early signs look promising. But here’s the trap that fools almost everyone: if you try enough strategies against past data, one of them will look brilliant purely by luck — the same way someone in a big enough crowd will flip ten heads in a row. So “it looked great on history” is, on its own, nearly worthless.

That’s why the system labels its edge plausible, but unconfirmed. Plausible because, even after subtracting a fair estimate of that luck, a small advantage still seems to be there. Unconfirmed because the only proof that really counts is results on days the system has never seen — and those aren’t in yet.

The honest rule

A test against the past can make almost anything look profitable. The only trustworthy test is forward — on real days the system never got to peek at first.

Go deeper — the numbers behind “plausible”

After a statistical haircut for picking the best-looking option out of many (“selection-deflation”), the estimated advantage lands around a few dollars per trade — but that figure is an upper bound, not a verdict. It only rules out one specific kind of luck.

Three larger doubts stay open: fine-tuning hidden inside each candidate, a subtle “we couldn’t actually have known to pick these names back then” problem, and the fact that every test against the same history spends down a limited budget of honest comparisons.

What “the fill” is.

A “fill” is just slang for a trade actually going through — you wanted to buy, and you got it, at some price. The “first fill” (the project calls it tranche-zero) is the very first one done with real money: a single contract, placed by hand, watched the whole time.

Why would one tiny trade matter so much? Because every test up to now assumed the system could buy and sell at the prices it saw on the screen. Reality charges a quiet toll: by the time your order actually reaches the market, the price may have drifted, so you pay a little more than you hoped. No amount of testing against history can measure that toll for you — only a real order can.

So the first fill answers one specific question that no backtest ever could: when the system thinks it can trade at a price, can it really?

What it is — and isn’t

It is not a verdict on whether the system makes money. It’s one honest data point about whether the real world behaves like the model. It happens to be the single most informative thing the project can do right now.

Go deeper — the toll, and why it’s being re-measured

The “toll” has two parts: the delay between deciding and executing (latency) and the price you give up crossing the spread (slippage). An early estimate of the delay turned out to be partly a measurement quirk, so that number is currently being recomputed rather than trusted.

What does survive: a roughly fixed cost hurts far more on cheap contracts than expensive ones — which points toward only trading above a certain price floor. That’s a refinement, not a reason to scrap the approach. See What’s Measured and the live Current State.

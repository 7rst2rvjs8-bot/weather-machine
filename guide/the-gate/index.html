What the “4/4 gate” actually means.

Imagine you build a machine that’s meant to trade for you. Before you ever let it near real money, you’d want to be certain of one boring but crucial thing: that the machine does exactly what it claims, every single time, with no glitches. The “gate” is that safety check.

So what does it actually check? Boring, essential stuff: that every trade gets recorded correctly, that the price information feeding the machine arrives clean and on time, that the software’s actions match its own instructions, and that nothing quietly breaks while no one is watching.

It has to do all of that perfectly, for four separate trading days. “4/4” just means four clean days out of four. It passed all four. That’s the whole phrase.

The part people get wrong

Passing the gate proved the machine is trustworthy. It did not prove the machine makes money. Those are two completely different questions — and never confusing them is the entire point.

Here’s why that distinction matters so much. It’s tempting to see a system that “works” and assume it must be profitable. But “the plumbing works” and “the strategy actually earns” are different claims. You first make sure your measuring instrument is accurate; only then can you trust what it tells you. The gate certifies the instrument. Whether there’s real money to be made — what this project calls the edge — is still an open question, on purpose.

Go deeper — the technical version

In the project’s terms, the gate certifies the harness — the execution, logging, and test scaffolding around the strategy — across consecutive clean, unattended trading days measured against pre-committed criteria (criteria fixed in advance, so the bar can’t move to fit the result).

What it explicitly does not certify is the edge: a clean instrument can faithfully measure an edge that isn’t there. Keeping “is the instrument trustworthy?” strictly apart from “is there an edge?” is the discipline the whole system is built around — see The Machine.
